    History of Palestinian Hummus

    The region of the southern Levant has a varied past and as such, its cuisine has contributions from various cultures.
    After the area originally inhabited by Jews, Edomites, Moabites and Ammonites was conquered by the Muslims in the 7th century CE,
    it became part of a Bilad al-Sham under the name Jund Filastin. Therefore, many aspects of Palestinian cuisine are similar to the cuisine of Syria—especially in the Galilee.
    Modern Syrian-Palestinian dishes have been generally influenced by the rule of three major Islamic groups: the Arabs, the Persian-influenced Arabs (Iraqis) and the Turks.[1]
    The Arabs that conquered Syria and Palestine initially had simple culinary traditions primarily based on the use of rice, lamb, yogurt and dates.
    [2] This cuisine did not advance for centuries until the rise of the Abbasid Caliphate, that established Baghdad as its capital and integrated elements of Persian cuisine into the existing Arab cuisine.
    [1] The Jerusalemite geographer al-Muqaddasi said this of Palestine's foods:
    From Palestine comes olives, dried figs, raisins, the carob fruit... from Jerusalem comes cheeses and the celebrated raisins of the species known as Ainuni and Duri, excellent apples...
    also pine nuts of the kind called 'Kuraish-bite', and their equal is not found elsewhere... from Sughar and Baysan come dates, the treacle called Dibs.[3]
    The cuisine of the Ottoman Empire—which incorporated Palestine in 1516—was partially made up of what had become, by then a "rich" Arab cuisine. After the Crimean War,
    in 1855, many other communities including Bosnians, Greeks, French and Italians began settling in the area especially in urban centers such as Jerusalem, Jaffa and Bethlehem. These communities' cuisines contributed to the character of Palestinian cuisine,
    especially communities from the Balkans.[1][4]
    Until around the 1950s–1960s, the main ingredients for rural Palestinian cuisine were olive oil,
    oregano and bread baked in a simple oven called a taboon.[5] Author G. Robinson Lees, writing in 1905,
    observed that "The oven is not in the house, it has a building of its own, the joint property of several families whose duty is to keep it always hot."[6]

    History of Palestinian Maqluba

    Maqluba (Arabic: مقلوبة), which means "upside-down" in Arabic,
    is a traditional Palestinian dish that dates back centuries and is beloved across the Levant.
    Known for its distinctive layered look, maqluba is made by carefully layering meat, rice, and vegetables,
    and then cooking them together in a pot.
    Once ready, the pot is flipped upside down onto a large serving platter,
    showcasing its colorful and flavorful layers.


    Historically, maqluba was originally prepared to celebrate the bountiful harvest and as a dish for special occasions.
    It's often made with lamb or chicken, accompanied by eggplant, cauliflower, and sometimes potatoes.
    Every region and household has its own variation of maqluba,
    making it a deeply personal and culturally significant meal.


    Maqluba is a symbol of hospitality in Palestine.
    It is traditionally served when hosting guests, and families often gather around it to enjoy its hearty flavors.
    Some historical records even suggest that maqluba was mentioned as early as the 13th century,
    with its origins believed to have been influenced by various Middle Eastern and Mediterranean cuisines.


    Over time, this iconic dish has become not just a meal, but a cherished tradition,
    and a source of pride in Palestinian homes.
    Maqluba is more than just food;
    it represents resilience, community, and a connection to Palestinian heritage.

    History of Palestinian Musakhan

    Musakhan (Arabic: مسخّن) is a classic Palestinian dish that is both simple and deeply flavorful.
    Known for its fragrant spices,
    musakhan consists of roasted chicken coated in sumac, onions, and spices, served on top of taboon bread.
    This bread, traditionally baked in a clay oven, soaks up the juices and spices,
    making it an essential part of the dish.
    The history of musakhan is intertwined with the Palestinian olive harvest season.
    This dish is often prepared in the fall,
    celebrating the freshly pressed olive oil and sumac from local harvests.
    These ingredients are essential to musakhan, giving it a unique,
    tangy flavor that highlights the produce of the Palestinian countryside.
    Musakhan has become a symbol of Palestinian pride and hospitality.
    It is typically served at large gatherings and celebrations, where it can be enjoyed communally.
    The dish also holds historical significance,
    as it reflects the agrarian lifestyle of many Palestinian villages,
    where olive trees and sumac bushes are integral parts of the landscape.

    History of Palestinian Mujaddara

    Mujaddara (Arabic: مجدرة) is a traditional Palestinian and Levantine dish made primarily from lentils, rice (or bulgur), and caramelized onions.
    Often referred to as “the dish of the poor,”
    mujaddara has been a staple in the region for centuries due to its simple ingredients, rich nutrients, and affordability.
    The origins of mujaddara are ancient, with some sources dating the dish back to medieval times in the Middle East.
    It was widely consumed as a humble yet nourishing meal, especially by those working the land.
    In Palestine, mujaddara is commonly prepared with rice,
    though in other Levantine regions, bulgur may be used instead.
    The dish is typically enjoyed warm in winter or served cold in summer with yogurt or salad.
    Besides being affordable and nutritious, mujaddara has cultural significance.
    It represents frugality and resourcefulness,
    embodying the Palestinian value of making the most out of available resources.
    For many, mujaddara is a comfort food that brings back memories of family gatherings and traditional village life.

    History of Palestinian Maftoul

    Maftoul, also known as Palestinian couscous, has been a beloved staple in Palestine for generations. Its unique hand-rolled preparation,
    made from bulgur and whole wheat flour, distinguishes it from other couscous varieties. Maftoul preparation was a social activity in Palestinian villages,
    where women would gather to roll and dry the grains, a process that could take hours.
    The dish is often cooked with vegetables or chicken and flavored with traditional spices, making it a cherished and comforting meal, especially in winter.
    It reflects the connection Palestinians have with their land, using locally sourced ingredients like olive oil, grains, and vegetables.

    Mahashi (Arabic: محشي) refers to a variety of stuffed vegetables that are popular in Palestinian cuisine.
